# ARVES — Success

## North Star

> **A stranger creates real economic value on ARVES — downloads it, builds something,
> publishes it, gets paid — without ever talking to the people who made it.**

Everything else is a proxy for this one sentence. When it starts happening, ARVES stops
being a project and becomes an ecosystem.

## The five events that matter

Success is not measured in commits or tests anymore. It is measured by whether these happen,
in roughly this order — none of which our own code can cause:

1. **First independent developer** installs ARVES and reaches a first success — *with no help
   from us.*
2. **First independent capability** is published to the Marketplace by an outside author.
3. **First independent runtime** earns certification, built by a team we have never met.
4. **First commercial product** is sold on ARVES.
5. **First company** adopts ARVES as its own platform — and the **first outside maintainer**
   contributes to the core.

**Today, all five are at zero.** That is honest and expected: everything proven so far is
grade **G1** (produced inside this program). These five events are the **G2** reality — the
platform proving it can live independently of its makers. They are the whole game now.

## The single metric

**Independent Value Creation.** Someone downloads → builds → publishes → earns, without ever
contacting the creators. Stars, downloads, and revenue are lagging indicators of this; this is
the thing itself.

## What success is *not*

Not `cargo test`. Not lines of code. Not a bigger runtime, a new framework, or another
methodology. After the seal, more core engineering is *diminishing returns*. Success is other
people creating value on a stable center.

## The compass rule

Every proposed investment answers one question:

> *Does this make an independent developer's first success faster or more likely?*

If the answer isn't a clear yes, it waits. This file is the team's compass; when in doubt,
re-read the North Star.
